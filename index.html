Power of Evidence-based Policymaking

  • EBP utilizes evidence from research to inform the policymaking process.
  • EBP has become increasingly important in the last few decades:
    • In the U.K., the emphasis on EBP started to deepen in 1997 (Sutcliffe & Court, 2005).
    • In the U.S., the Obama administration developed and implemented six evidence-based initiatives, covering various areas of social intervention (Haskins & Baron, 2011).
    • Research organizations published papers highlighting the role of EBP as the crucial factor in the success of Congress’ spending decisions and federal social programs (Baron & Haskins, 2011; Liebman, 2013; Muhlhausen, 2015).
  • Advantage of EBP: its methodical approach to policymaking
  • The advantage of EBP could be shown in three aspects (Baron & Haskins, 2011; Liebman, 2013; Muhlhausen, 2015):
    • Social benefit: by collecting high-quality evidence on the effectiveness of social programs.
    • Economic benefit: expanding effective social programs will help fully utilize any untapped human potential, leading to faster economic growth (Cohen & Soto, 2007; Liebman, 2013; Mankiw, Romer, & Weil, 1992).
    • Fiscal benefit: shutting down ineffective programs will help reduce debts in the long run.
  • EBP can also be applied to countries in transition, with greater potential for improvement.
  • Issues with applying EBP to countries in transition:
  • political instability,
    • conflict,
    • academic freedom,
    • media freedom ,
    • and civil society
