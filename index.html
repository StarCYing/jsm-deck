Extreme Poverty and Hunger

  • The UN MDG to Eradicate Extreme Poverty and Hunger had three different targets. The first target was to halve the proportion of people living on less than $1.25/day.
  • Although the number of people living in extreme poverty globally fell by 1 billion people, the world’s extremely poor people are distributed unevenly across two regions: South Asia and Sub Saharan Africa. This can be seen in the data visualization on the left, with Sub-Saharan Africa showing the least progress.
  • Additionally, women face a greater risk of living in poverty. As the data visualization on the right shows, in 41 countries women are more likely to live in poor households than men.
  • The second target was to gain full and productive employment for all people.
  • However, the data visualization on the left shows there is a lack of employment opportunities in comparison to the growing labor force. Young women especially are disproportionately affected by this problem. Again, we see this issue of gender inequality in relation to poverty.
  • Although the proportion of workers living in extreme poverty has largely decreased, nearly half the population of employed people are still working in vulnerable conditions, as shown in the data visualization on the right.
  • The third target was to halve the proportion of people suffering from hunger by 2015.
  • As seen in the data visualization to the left, the proportion of undernourished people in developing regions has decreased by nearly half.
  • However, differences in hunger prevalences continue to exist across regions. The data visualization on the right portrays that the pace of reduction in caribbean, oceania, southern asia, and sub-saharan regions has been too slow to reach target.
  • Overall, much progress has been made to decrease the proportion of people living in extreme poverty and hunger. However, in order to achieve the goal of complete eradication, the world faces many obstacles and challenges.

  • Simply put, EBP utilizes evidence from research to inform the policymaking process.
  • In the last few decades, EBP has becoming increasingly important.
  • Advantage of EBP: its methodical approach to policymaking
  • The advantage of EBP could be shown in three aspects:
    • social benefit
    • economic benefit
    • fiscal benefit
  • The power of EBP lies in its methodical approach to policymaking, an approach that rendered opinion-based policymaking ineffective. According to the literature, this advantage of EBP over the obsolete opinion-based policymaking could be shown in three aspects: social, economic, and fiscal benefits.
  • First, the social benefit of EBP could be reaped by collecting high-quality evidence on the effectiveness of social programs. Scientifically rigorous evaluations of programs would reveal programs that work, as well as those that fail. Policymakers can then eliminate programs with mediocre results, and expand programs that are effective.
  • Second, expanding effective social programs will in turn lead to significant economic results. For example, if we expand programs that has empirically improved the education of low-income children, we will eventually close the income-based academic-achievement gaps, and fully utilize any untapped human potential. Utilizing the human resources will lead to faster economic growth.
  • The third benefit of EBP is the fiscal benefit. In 2015, the U.S. federal government owed more than $18.1 trillion in debt (Muhlhausen, 2015). Replacing ineffective programs with effective ones will reduce government spending and debts. Shutting down ineffective programs will improve the allocation of scarce federal resources, and help reduce debts in the long run.
  • EBP can also be applied to countries in transition, with greater potential for improvement.
  • Issues with applying EBP to countries in transition:
    • political instability,
    • conflict,
    • academic freedom,
    • media freedom,
    • and civil society
  • Many EBP strategies of developed countries can be applied to countries in transition.
  • Since EBP is often less developed in these countries, the potential for growth is greater; in consequence, the improvement could be much more significant.

Data Visualization:

“the use of computer-supported interactive visual representations of abstract data to amplify cognition”

- Card, Mackinlay, Shneiderman (1999)
  • Data visualization can be understood as the use of electronic tools to promote accurate comprehension of data.
  • Under this definition, the role of data visualization is two-fold. First, it helps viewers achieve a clear understanding of the data. Second, it is researchers’ instrument to communicate their messages about the data to the viewers.
  • In view of the aforementioned role of data visualization, we present several principles of data visualization, which were discussed by author Stephen Few in his article “Information Visualization, Design, and the Arts: Collision or Collaboration”. We illustrate these principles by examples of DVs that we came across during our lit review.
  • We apply these principles in our research, so that our data visualizations facilitate understanding of data from the UN.

Quantitative values should be displayed using the lengths of simple, two-dimensional objects.
  • First, viewers can easily understand quantitative values if they are displayed using the lengths of simple, two-dimensional objects, such as bars.
  • This method prompts the human mind to make comparisons on a one-dimensional plane (the lengths) while objects are presented on a two-dimensional surface.
  • On the contrary, angles and areas require the human brain to make two-dimensional comparisons in a two-dimensional presentation; therefore, they tend to make it more difficult for viewers to decode the visualizations.
  • For example, “North Korea Tuberculosis” displays the presence of TB in the DPRK in 1990-2012 using a simple bar graph.[1] The difference in the lengths of the bars stands out, immediately drawing the viewers’ attention to the rapid increase in TB prevalence in the DPRK.

DVs that encourage viewers’ engagement with information tend to be more successful at promoting viewers’ learning (Few, 2011).   • People use two systems when thinking:

Chronic malnutrition (stunting)

  • Defined from the height of age (H/A) index, expressed in z-score (HAZ)
  • HAZ represents the number of standard deviations units from the median of the WHO 2006 reference population.
  • Global stunting is defined as HAZ < -2 SD.
  • Moderate stunting is defined as HAZ < -2 SD and >= -3 SD.
  • Severe stunting is defined as HAZ < -3 SD.
  • Stunting is irreversible after 24 months of age.
  • We observe that the provinces that suffer from higher prevalence of malnutrition, such as Ryanggang, Jagang, and Kangwon, are mountainous provinces.
  • On the contrary, provinces with lower prevalence such as Pyongyang, South Hwanghae, and South Phyongan enjoy a sea-level structure.

Acute malnutrition (wasting)

  • Estimated from the weight for height (W/H) index, expressed in z-score (WHZ)
  • WHZ represents the number of standard deviations units from the median of the WHO 2006 reference population.
  • Global wasting is defined as WHZ < -2 SD.
  • Moderate wasting is defined as WHZ < -2 SD and >= -3 SD.
  • Severe wasting is defined as WHZ < -3 SD.

Net Migration Exceeding 1000

  • In the previous DV series, we mentioned that citizens of Ryanggang have the worst health conditions but appear immobile. We plotted global chronic malnutrition in children aged 24-59.9 months as a heat map.
  • The arrows represent net migration; their widths are scaled to size.
  • Viewers can click and drag each province, if they wish to examine the province and its net migration.
  • Takeaway: provinces with the worst malnutrition, namely Jagang and Ryanggang, appear isolated. These provinces have little migration, and their citizens mainly migrate to Pyongyang. A pressing observation arises: citizens of these areas are suffering from stunting, yet they are unable to escape that situation.
  • Malnutrition in children can have damaging long term consequences, as it affects the future generation of a nation. In the interactive visualization “Scaling Malnutrition to the DPRK”, we allow users to choose to display chronic or acute malnutrition (or both) on a map of the DPRK.
  • Chronic malnutrition, or Stunting (red). The prevalence of chronic malnutrition were scaled against the total landmass of each province. Three levels of stunting are displayed, severe being the darkest shade of red, followed by moderate, and global has the lightest shade of red. Scale the coordinates of each province boundary by the square root of the prevalence of each province.
  • Wasting (blue). Prevalence of wasting is also displayed as land areas. Three levels of severity of wasting are displayed: the deepest shade of blue is severe, followed by moderate, and global has the lightest shade of blue.
  • Takeaways: first, Each type of malnutrition takes up a considerable amount of the landmass in each province (show red, then blue). Thus we should treat malnutrition for all provinces, not just the capital.
  • second, global and moderate acute malnutrition show very little difference (show blue). The boundaries of global and moderate wasting almost perfectly coincide. A child that is afflicted with global acute malnutrition is actually already under moderate acute malnutrition. The implication of this trend is significant for non-governmental organizations and humanitarian efforts: In order to tackle acute malnutrition, we must simultaneously face global and moderate cases.
